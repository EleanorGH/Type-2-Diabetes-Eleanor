Type 2 Diabetes



What is type 2 diabetes?

Type 2 diabetes mellitus, also known as type 2 diabetes, is a metabolic disorder in which the pancreas doesnâ€™t make enough insulin and/or the cells become resistant to insulin. Unlike type 1 diabetes, type 2 can be managed with changes to your diet and exercise.

Insulin is a hormone produced by the pancreas, a large gland located in your abdomen behind your stomach, that regulates the glucose levels in your blood.

Risk Factors

Factors that could contribute to someone getting type 2 diabetes include:

  • being overweight or obese
  • having high blood pressure
  • having a family history of diabetes

How to reduce your risk:

Lose Weight

You may be able to prevent or delay diabetes by losing 5 to 7 percent of your starting weight.
Move More

Get at least 30 minutes of physical activity five days a week.
Eat Mostly Healthy Foods

Try and reduce calories, for example, water instead of sweetened drinks, and less fatty foods.

Glycemic Index

The glycemic index (or GI) is a ranking of carbohydrates on a scale of 0 to 100 according to how much they raise blood sugar (glucose) levels after eating.

Foods that are quickly digested, absorbed and metabolised are high GI foods.
Low GI foods, which produce smaller fluctations in blood glucose and insulin levels, reduce your risk of type 2 diabetes and is key to maintaining weight loss.

Fibre

Fibre is the indigestible parts of plant foods, such as vegetables, fruits, grains, beans and legumes. It is type of a carbohydrate that helps keep our digestive systems healthy. It is important for digestive health and regular bowel movement.

Types of Fibre Include:

Soluble Fibre

Helps to slow the emptying process in the stomach, which helps you feel fuller. It also helps to lower cholesterol and stabilise blood glucose levels. It is found in fruits, vegetables, oats, barley and legumes.
Insoluble Fibre

Absorbs water to help to soften the contents of the bowels and support regular bowel movements. It also helps to keep us full and keep the bowel environment healthy. It is found in wholegrain breads and cereals, nuts, seeds, wheat bran and the skin of fruit and vegetables.
Resistant Starch

Is not digested in the small intestine and instead goes to the large intestine where it can assist in the production of good bacteria and improves bowel health. It is found in undercooked pasta, under-ripe bananas, in cooked and cooled potato, and in rice.

Fibre, soluble fibre in particuar, can slow the absorption of sugar and help improve blood sugar levels. A healthy diet that includes soluble and insoluble fibre may reduce the risk of developing type 2 diabetes.

High-fibre foods also help with weight loss, as they tend to be more filling than low-fibre foods, so you're likely to eat less and stay satisfied longer. High-fibre foods tend to take longer to eat and be less 'energy dense', which means they have fewer calories for the same volume of food.




















High-Fibre Burger


Tips for Making a Successful Burger:

  1. Indent the top of the patties:

    2. If you use your thumb to indent the top of the patties before cooking, as it's cooking and the juices try to escape they'll pool in the dent, and get reabsorbed instead of running down the side and getting lost.

  2. Don't press down on patties while cooking:

    3. Pressing down on patties while cooking will force out all the natural juices, and will make the patties dry.

  3. Use fresh tomatoes and crisp lettuce:

    4. The fresher the ingredients the more refreshing and tasty the burger. Always put the tomato on top and the lettuce on the bottom, so that it can catch some of the juices going through the burger.

My high-fibre burger is of a simple deisign. Encased in a wholegrain bun are, from top to bottom, a quarter of a grated carrot, a few slices of tomato, a lettuce leaf and a beef patty with tomato sauce spread on top of it. Last but certainly not least, underneath the patty is another lettuce leaf to catch the juices running through the burger!

The wholegrain bun contains all three mentioned fibres: soluble, insoluble, and resistant starch.
The grated carrot contains soluble and insoluble fibre, while the
tomato contains mainly soluble fibre.
The lettuce leaves contains some soluble but mainly insoluble fibre, and
the tomato sauce, like the tomato, contains mainly soluble fibre.
The beef patty contains no fibre, as it is made of eggs and meat, both of which have little to no fibre content.
